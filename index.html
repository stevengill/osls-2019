Our journey to Open Source

In late 2016, we started investigating what Open Source looked like across the company.
Where to start? First, we did an audit of all the OS resources/programs and we wanted to hear from developers

Adobe Open Source Office


- We formed the OSO at the end of 2016.
- It was a grass roots type effort, with us only working on it part time.
- The original members were essentially volunteers as they had other responsibilities as part of their day jobs.
- The thing that brought them together was a shared love of open source and their desire to have Adobe give back to the community.
- OSO had one clear goal and that was to help grow OS at Adobe. Help teams open source projects and contribute back.
Adobe is traditionally siloed One huge benefit is our OSO is composed of a group of developers, marketing and legal. Very rare to have a cross organizational team like us in the company. We sit under the developer relations org. Would be more ideal if we sat under the CEOs office. Our role is more about bringing people together. We see ourselves as more of a central team to all of Adobe.

Wild West on GitHub

  • 35 Adobe related GitHub Orgs
  • Repos abandoned, issues and PRs ignored
- no guidance on how best to utilize GitHub
- the easiest way forward was for teams to create and manage their own org. We ended up with 35 Adobe related GitHub orgs.
- With no commitment to continue to support, many of those 35 orgs were abondanded without any notice. Sad to see issues and pull requests ignored for years. No central authority to provide guidelines around archiving these repos.

Revamped OS Submission Process

The next obvious thing to tackle was the submission process! So we started to gather all of the requirements from all of the various teams involved in the OS process. We took those requirements and created a checklist for teams to follow.
- We went back and forth on what tool to use to manage the process.
- We settled on creating a git repo on our internal github. We put the checklist of all of the requirements into a issue_template.
- Every submitter was required to create an issue in our repo and start filling out the checklist in the issue template.
- One big benefit of this new process meant that it was open and transparent. Anyone could view previous submissions and see how they went. We wanted to remove the mystery behind the approvals.
- Over time and many discussions with various stakeholders, the list grew to 53 items. The process was still daunting and a little confusing.
- Our plan was for the checklist to be a first pass. It was quick to get up and it allowed us to build relationships with the stakeholders. It gave us at the open source office time to learn the nuances of contributing to and open sourcing code.
- So earlier this year, from our learnings, we launched the new open source submission form.
The new submission form is radically simpler.
- We decided to remove the ambiguous questions for more simple and direct ones - easier to understand for developers (i.e. not written by lawyers).
- We created categorizes of open source projects that the open source office would be able to approve without going through all of the stakeholders.
- It bolted on top of the existing process - Users logged in with their enterprise Git account, and issues were submitted to the existing issue tracker. No surprises for anynone already involved, just a nice UI on top of existing process.

New Approvelist and Denylist

We also created a approvelist of previously approved projects as well as a denylist of projects that were out of bounds.
- This means if one employee received approval for contributing to a project, all other Adobe employees could do so as well without needing to fill out forms or ask for permission.
- We integratated these lists right into our submission form to give instant approvals or denials. This significantly sped up our approval times.

Executive Buy In

  • Get creative
  • Tie Open Source to a pressing business need
  • Identify enthusiasts
Fixing internal perception boils down to executive support and buy in
- This might sound familiar to those in OS program offices
- We tried a myriad of things. When we first started out we got fast "no's" it was too risky, too time consuming, etc. so we got creative about the way we talked about Open Source
- We tied OS to a pressing business need - for Adobe, that is becoming a platform company and what good is a platform without developers building on it?
- We positioned OS as a great channel to connect to a customer segment that is a growing demographic at Adobe, the developer
- We positioned OS as a way to lend transparency and authenticity to our developer offerings - We positioned it as a way to gain credibility - Lastly, we found enthusiasts across the company, leaders who understood and were enthusiastic about OS and had them blog consistently, speak at our Open Source Summits, recognize employees in their org, and test out different programs we'd come up with

Top 25 company at GitHub

Lastly, a single, clear goal that is easy to get people behind and helps get executive buy in. Something that is easy to talk about and quantify
- This was to become a top 25 contributing company on GitHub

OSS-Contributors project

  • Scan GitHub every month to get company-to-user associations as reported by people's Company field on their GitHub profile
  • We cross-reference that with commit activity on GitHub
  • Original Idea from a blog post by Felipe Hoffa, Developer Advocate at Google
Fil: we have yet to open source this project, but it's an attempt at quantifying our ranking among other companies.
- every month we pound the github REST API and keep track of user to company associations in our own DB
- we cross reference this information with commit activity on GitHub
- we stole Felipe Hoffa's approach from his blog post on this topic, and expanded on it a bit
- there are many flaws of this approach: we only count commits as contributions (whereas we want to count comments, PRs, reviews, etc.). the company-to-user associations are rough at best. but that's ok.
