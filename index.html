Our journey to Open Source

In late 2016, we started investigating what Open Source looked like across the company.
- Where to start? First, we did an audit of all the OS resources/programs/successful pockets.
- We wanted to hear from developers

Employees hated the process

  • Legal became the "boogyman"
  • Started pushing to GitHub without approval (Big Risk)
- gave up or avoided the existing process

Wild West on GitHub

  • 36 Adobe related GitHub Orgs
  • Repos abandoned, issues and PRs ignored
- no guidance on how best to utilize GitHub
- the easiest way forward was for teams to create and manage their own org. We ended up with 35 Adobe related GitHub orgs.
- With no commitment to continue to support, many of those 35 orgs were abandoned without any notice.
- Sad to see issues and pull requests ignored for years. No central authority to provide guidelines around archiving these repos.

Revamped OS Submission Process

The first obvious thing to tackle was the submission process! So we started to gather all of the requirements from all of the various teams involved in the OS process. We took those requirements and created a checklist for teams to follow.
The new submission form is radically simpler.
- We decided to remove the ambiguous questions for more simple and direct ones - easier to understand for developers (i.e. not written by lawyers).
- We created categories of open source submissions that would require different levels of approval.
- Some that would only require the OSO approval, some that would require all stakeholders.
- If there was one needing conflict of interest review (that the OSO can do) they wouldn't need to fill out the entire 53 item checklist.
- It bolted on top of the existing process - Users logged in with their enterprise Git account, and issues were submitted to the existing issue tracker. No surprises for anyone already involved, just a nice UI on top of existing process.

New Approvelist and Denylist

We also created a approvelist of previously approved projects as well as a denylist of projects that were out of bounds.
- This means if one employee received approval for contributing to a project, all other Adobe employees could do so as well without needing to fill out forms or ask for permission.
- We integratated these lists right into our submission form to give instant approvals or denials. This significantly sped up our approval times.

OSS-Contributors project

  • Scan GitHub every month to get company-to-user associations as reported by people's Company field on their GitHub profile
  • We cross-reference that with commit activity on GitHub
  • Original Idea from a blog post by Felipe Hoffa, Developer Advocate at Google
  • https://github.com/adobe/oss-contributors
We have yet to open source this project, but it's an attempt at quantifying our ranking among other companies.
- every month we pound the github REST API and keep track of user to company associations in our own DB
- we cross reference this information with commit activity on GitHub
- we stole Felipe Hoffa's (dev advocate at Google) approach from his blog post on this topic, and expanded on it a bit
- In 2017, we were ranked 56th with 38 unique contributors across 55 repositories
- Only 38 contributors?! Original approach used GitHub user's git configuration email, but who puts their company email in their git config?
- Now we use the "company field" in GitHub profiles and use our internal communication channels to encourage employees to add Adobe to their company field
- Beginning of 2018 we sat at 34th with 115 unique contributors to projects w at least one star
- Now we sit at 14th, thanks in no small part to the acquisition of Magento that has a HUGE Open Source community
- there are many flaws of this approach: we only count commits as contributions (whereas we want to count comments, PRs, reviews, etc.). the company-to-user associations are rough at best. but that's ok.
- Progress is progress and small incrememtal changes are still moving us in the right direction

Get creative

  • Change the narrative
  • Tie Open Source to a pressing business need
  • Create case studies
  • Enlist allies (HR)
  • Identify enthusiasts
  • Make it as easy as possible to contribute
- Like I said, our narrative was originally that OS saved development time, created better software, etc. and we still got fast no's - So we got creative and changed the narrative. We talked about OS as another channel to engage with developers.
- We talked about OS as a way to lend legitimacy and authenticity to our developer offerings
- We found teams who had benefited in some ways from Open Source and talked about those constantly
- We talked to HR about attracting and retaining talent through OS.
- And we found and leveraged enthusiasts from around the company. Employees, especially executives, who understood and were enthusiastic about OS. We now have several executives that blog consistently, speak at our conferences, recognize employees in their orgs and demo new programs for the OSO.
